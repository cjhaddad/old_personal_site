Understanding the Source of Conflict

March 29, 2015

Not too long ago, I found myself in my car driving on the wrong side of a parking lot drive aisle. I stopped, put the car in park, and got out of the car. In a huff I walked around the parking lot ticket machine's barrier and began pressing the ticket button, in an attempt to get the barrier to go up. A security guard and parking lot attendant walked up behind me, one with a phone in their hand looking as if they were getting ready to dial.

"Why isn't this going up?" "There needs to be the weight of the car in front of it." "Please make it go up! I am trying to leave!"

This scenario, where I was making a bona-fide ass out of myself, began when the parking lot ticket machine was prompting me to pay $19 to exit. I had just spent a little over three hours at a doctor's appointment and getting x-rays on my shoulder and wrists. The $19 expense came as a surprise to me, since I had my parking ticket validated by my doctor's office. Long story short, I asked the parking lot attendant why it was asking me to pay $19. She said that since I had stayed over 3 hours the validation no longer was valid. She wasn't budging. I yelled, explaining to her how I felt this rule did not take into account the length needed to attend two separate appointments. Needless to say, in my upset state I found myself attempting to avoid paying the $19 by trying to drive around the barrier, move the barrier on the entrance side, and so on. I ended up paying the $19...It took driving less than a block down the road for my anger to turn to straight-up embarrassment and confusion as to why I acted like such a jerk.

The emotion underlying this incident was fear. Unfeigned fear of financial instability. This incident occurred a couple of months ago, when I was at the beginning of knowingly living off of a finite amount of savings. My last paycheck was in January and the next one was (is) TBD. I was at the beginning of a new career journey and identity shift, where I had recently finished graduate school and just started digging into Dev Bootcamp's Phase 0 curriculum. Money was on my mind. And money was on my mind, because a deep-seated fear that often arises within me is to not have enough...which is intrinsically tied to not being enough. Life was/ is changing (as it always is). My identity as a graduate student and a member of an academic community was ending. While also exciting, I was disassociating myself from a community to which I had been a part for more than 7 years. Adding further tumult to this shift, consistent deposits of paychecks in my bank account for a foreseeable future were ending. And on top of all of this underlying emotion, I had just confronted at the doctor's office my immortality, the physical pain of injury, and the uncertainty of a path to healing. The mind/body/emotion connection behind this scenario is so glaringly obvious.

So, back to that $19 that set me over the edge...was $19 going to break my bank account? No. When I returned home about 20 minutes later, I let myself digest what had just happened. Then, I emailed the parking garage manager (because of course I demanded that the parking lot attendant give me their info). I apologized, sincerely. I expressed my concern with their policy, -- since there are multiple doctor's offices in the attached building, and to obtain proper medical care, one could easily spend more than three hours waiting for and receiving treatment. I insisted that a refund for my $19 be issued, and that the refund go to the parking lot attendant who was at the receiving end of my anger.

If I could go back, I would have never yelled at this woman, nor would I had made a scene by trying to exit using the entrance. Especially, I would take back yelling. I would have asked for a manager's name to express my concern, but I would have also paid the $19 and be on my way. No big deal. Stepping back to engage with the underlying emotional currents of this incident gives me a more in-depth perspective of understanding my behavior and reactions. Yelling like I did felt downright awful. Aggressive behavior of any variety is not something I think of when I think of myself, or who I want to be. I strive to practice assertiveness that is infused with patience, understanding, and compassion -- for others, for self. Re-examining this incident allows me to see how my knee-jerk (emphasis on jerk) reactions were rooted in fear, and how in these types of moments stepping back to connect with my breath and current mental/ emotional state can prevent unnecessary conflict.
